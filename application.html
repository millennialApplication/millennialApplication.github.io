What does the refreshed Fonterra purpose mean to you?

As someone who believes in the values of community and constant improvement, my personal history is a living case in point of commitment and sacrifice in the short-term, resulting in long-term benefit. Having completed one degree and out in the workplace, I gave up a good job to return to study to enhance my skillset. This sacrifice resulted in me landing a great job in Fonterra in a field that I am passionate about, where I get to work with a highly skilled and motivated team to solve complex business problems that have a significant impact.

One of the things that I continue to struggle with about large corporations is the short-term focus they exhibit. Many businesses today look for short-term profits. This is a trend that has been spreading since the Reagan administration in the US. This kind of approach to gain the largest possible short-term returns is often at the expense of the long-term benefits to the shareholders.

With Fonterra, I saw something different. In addition to the benefits and opportunities associated with a large corporation, our profits supported rural farmers rather than lining the pockets of the wealthy. I wanted to be a part of this. This has the flow-on effect of supporting rural communities, which plays a big part in creating the New Zealand I know and love. The fact that we’re returning to a long-term value creation mantra appeals to me, as I’m sure it appeals to farmers with their inter-generational values. 

One of my favorite quotes is “What man is a man who does not make the world better.” I have benefited from others who have invested in me and I endeavour to find ways in which I can pass on what I have received, to give back. Working collaboratively with my colleagues, we strive to create ongoing value for our farmers. As a co-op, we create goodness for generations of our customers. Providing them with high quality, nutritious products enables them to do and be more. This takes the circle of benefit from us in Fonterra; our farmers, that we support; and extends it to the end users, that we enable. 

Change Maker

As a Millennial Council member, you have been given the task and responsibility to activate the new purpose for the entire oCFO community -- how would you go about doing this?

There are two avenues where I believe I can contribute to the implementation of the new strategy and purpose. During my normal day-to-day activities, there are a range of opportunities to help implement the new purpose. If a member of the Millennial Council, a host of new opportunities become available to make a larger impact and work with senior leaders to better the co-op.

Day-to-day Activities 

In the relatively short time at Fonterra, I have had the opportunity to develop a reading club. Bringing people together from different teams, the purpose of the club is to develop people’s critical thinking and analysis of different points of view. Having people from different backgrounds and experiences has been eye opening. It’s incredible to listen to the different insights people take out of reading books like The Black Swan (not the movie version). This is the type of endeavour that I’m motivated to be a part of. 

Millennial Plan

In addition to my day-to-day activities, I believe a two-pronged approach is needed to provide long term benefits from the refreshed purpose. The first prong would be a short-term information strategy, which is essential for informing people of where we are heading. I would also implement a long-term strategy, in order to fully recognize the potential of our new purpose. The second prong must take a long-term view as shifting deep rooted organizational culture can take a long time to authentically change.

The short-term informing process can be through multiple mediums. It can be dispersed from managers to their direct reports through the different layers of the organization. There could be a co-op wide email breaking down the different components of the new purpose. We could also organize a Miles’ update video to talk about what the new purpose means, how it aligns with who we are, and the benefits we can see from embracing it. This last part is crucial. Neurochemistry and behavioural research indicate that people need to see the benefit of the new purpose for them to fully embrace it. Otherwise, it could end up as lip service and a box to tick on the annual reviews.

To unleash the full benefits of the purpose, we must determine what we are currently doing that doesn’t align with our purpose. This would be developed in collaboration and insights from the senior leaders. Once these have been determined, we have three options. We can alter our behaviour, our purpose, or we can treat this as a north star that we move towards over time. One potential topic that I sense may fall under this category is a lack of trust within parts of Fonterra. If this lack of trust exists, it could undermine our purpose of “you, me, us, together”.  

“What are we currently doing that doesn’t align with our purpose?”

There have been a number of instances that make me concerned that we could lack trust. I’ve had teams say that they don’t want to share what they are working on, because it could be used against them. This can also be seen with the pull between self-interest and what’s best for the co-op. With a lack of trust, we act in self-interest rather than the good of the co-op. One example of this is people’s behaviour with the EOFY rapidly approaching and a strategy of trying to save as much EBIT as possible. I’ve observed multiple teams trying to push projects through, in order to spend their budget. Many of these projects could be postponed or done internally, saving on consultant fees. This is a warning sign of a culture that lacks trust that the co-op will take care of them if they put the co-op first in everything they do.

With an organization as large as ours, it will take time to build this trust. It starts at the top. But those at the top can’t create an environment of safety and trust for all those in the organization. Rooted in tribalism, psychology indicates that they can only do provide this feeling for approximately 150 people. However, they can make those close to them feel save and trusted. Those people, in turn, can extend the circle of safety to those around them. In this way, the trust can continue to spread throughout the co-op.

This would be a big change for a slow-moving co-op. But I believe it to be the right thing to do. Therefore, I would collaborate with both senior leaders and those around me to ask the question, develop the plan, and make the change. This top-down model of trust takes longer to build. However, it will be authentic and run deeper, becoming fully ingrained in our culture. Building this trust and co-operative spirit will allow us to build an environment where we are able to truly work you, me, us, together to generate goodness for generations to come. 

Community

With a strategy & purpose refresh, please illustrate the important role you see the oCFO community playing, and the contribution you see yourself making to this as a Millennial Council member

I believe the wider oCFO community has a massive part to play in developing and implementing both the short- and long-term strategy and purpose. The oCFO sits in a predominantly support role for many of the other parts of the business. We have an amazing opportunity to help implement the new strategy and purpose.

Our influence on implementing the strategy and purpose ranges from a high-level perspective through to the very granular. At the highest level, the strategy team sit at the core of continuing to flesh out the strategy. They play a key role in supporting the FMT to refine the strategy. We also have finance providing a financial framework to aid in decision making. At a more granular level, the Group Procurement team look after $6b in costs. Their decision making has a significant influence on EBIT. The DPAA team provide a deep dive into potential benefits and issues across the various parts of the co-op. There is also the GIS team, who’s synergistic collaboration with the rest of the co-op supports day-to-day operations, as well as having the possibility of improving efficiency and reducing costs going forward. While not directly responsible for producing our products, the oCFO has a critical part to play in the success or failure of the new strategy and purpose.

The broad range of my skillset and experiences provide an excellent foundation for the spectrum of work and potential topics covered in the LT agenda and ‘must win’ initiatives. I’ve spent time working with a successful high-tech firm, refining their business model and improving their processes. With a background in economics, management, and operations research, I have a strong theoretical underpinning for both high-level and in-depth analysis. I also have the people and communication skills to influence decision makers. When starting out my career as a hazardous area electrical engineer, I listened to the senior electrical engineer and hazardous zoning expert arguing for months about the right compliance systems and processes to implement. Taking positives and mitigating pitfalls from both points of view, I was able to present my ideas for an end-to-end redesign of the system and processes. They were both so happy with the idea that they put me in charge of implementing it.

As exemplified in the previous section, I believe my role as a member of the Millennial Council is to continue to think deeply about how our co-op operates and how it could be better. As a member of the council, I would be a part of a group taking a fresh look at problems, collaboratively bouncing ideas off each other, and fleshing out ideas into actionable plans to improve the co-op. This would involve asking the unconstrained mental questions, where we aren’t limited by the mental state of “this is how we’ve always done it.” We can ask “Why?”, which opens up new alternatives to consider. In working with senior leaders, we can apply the Socratic method to test both the current business model and our fresh new ideas.

There are a number of questions that I’ve already been asking that tie into our new strategy. People have spent a considerable amount of time looking at how we can better utilize our milk. My question would then be, “How can we utilize our other resources?” Can we increase plant utilization by processing other products? These could be things like drying pea protein, kale or spinach powders. Could we use our bottling plants to bottle water when we aren’t bottling milk for extended periods of time? Looking at our DC’s, could we sale storage space for the periods when we won’t be utilizing all the space? Could Fonterra backload products for 3rd parties on paid-for train carriages, rather than being the largest shipper of air in New Zealand? All of these are tough questions, with unclear answers at this stage. However, by taking a fresh look at our options, we can potentially increase our EBIT and leverage our core business model to create additional benefit for our farmers. 

Superpower

Possibly the nerdiest superpower of all time, I would say that it would be the power of problem solving through critical thinking. My costume would probably resemble that of the great Steve Urkle, which would be rather odd seeing as I’m reasonably tall.  

I have a keen ability to analyze a problem. This could be at a big picture, strategy level; all the way down to a granular problem. As a part of my solution, I take into consideration the potential issues with the solution. I try not to find the best solution, but the best solution that people are willing and able to adopt.
